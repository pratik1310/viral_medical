Pathology of Acute appendicitis
Obstructive Acute appendicitis:-
Lymphoid hyperplasia causes obstruction to the lumen of appendix
⬇️
Obstruction increase the severity of inflammatory process
⬇️
Closed loop continuing the normal secretion of appendicular mucosa
⬇️
Produces rapid distension
⬇️
Luminal capacity is 0.1ml
⬇️
Secretion of 0.5ml distal to the block raise the intraluminal pressure upto 50 cm of water
⬇️
Distension stimulates the visceral nerve ending concern with pain
⬇️
Vague , dull ,diffuse pain in umbilical and lower epigastric region
⬇️
Peristalsis is also stimulated and it causes cramping pain
⬇️
Rapid multiplication of residential bacteria
⬇️
Continued distension causes obstruction in the lymphatic drainage
⬇️
Bacteria passes into submucosa layer due to odema and ulceration
⬇️
Resolution may occur if the anti bacterial therapy is given
⬇️
If intraluminal pressure rises further it occludes venules and capillary but arterioles inflow is continues
⬇️
This causes engorgement and vascular congestion of appendix
⬇️
This stage there is reflux nausea and vomiting starts and visceral pain also becomes severe
⬇️
Gradually the serosa is also involved because presence of HIATUS MUSCULARIS and local peritonitis ensues
⬇️
There is shifting pain to the right lower quadrant
⬇️
Greater omentum and loops small bowel becomes adherent to this inflamed appendix and prevent further contamination
⬇️
This results in appendicular abscess
When the bacterial invasion occurs to deeper coats
⬇️
Fever , tachycardia, luecocytosis may develop
⬇️
This is due to absorption of bacterial toxin and dead tissue products
⬇️
Distension of appendix with pus is called empyema of appendix
⬇️
Peritonitis is the greatest threat of acute appendicitis and this may occur due to

- Free migration of bacteria through ischaemic appendicular wall
- Frank perforation
